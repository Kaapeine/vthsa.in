I'm constantly thinking about the future of work, like nearly everyone these days. AI seems like it's capable of automating a vast amount of white collar work, especially the kind of work that amounts to intellectual manual labour.

For a software engineer, that includes tasks like writing APIs, wiring up UI, and a large number of other everyday tasks that people used to be paid by the hour for. Figuring out arcane dependency and package management issues. Debugging syntax errors. The list goes on. A lot of these kinds of tasks can be done by AI coding agents fairly well now. It doesn't matter if they don't get it 100% right the first time, a single engineer who knows what good output looks like can slowly prompt their way into the correct solution. You can be as slow or fast as you want with AI, all this talk of one-shotting things and having agents work in parallel is a distraction in my opinion. But even going slow, which is what I try to do, the speed up in productivity is enormous.

But if AI can do so much of the small stuff by itself, and it pretty much has access to all knowledge ever written down, it genuinely re-adjusts the value provided by a single engineer. There are so many jobs where the whole point was doing the kind of plumbing work I talked about earlier. And there used to be a lot of value in being a digital plumber and knowing your pipes well. But now, what is the point? Learning exact API and syntax of every framework and library you touch (the kind you see padding every fullstack developer's resume) seems like a waste of time when AI can just handle it for you. I think digital plumbing work is going to be increasingly deprioritized now and that's a good thing. 

People can and should focus on the bigger picture now. Leave the small stuff to the AI. Learn concepts in as much depth as possible.  That depth is what lets you direct AI effectively and recognize when it's wrong. I think building a solid conceptual understanding of everything and developing a strong intuition is the only way forward. The only things that matter are having good ideas and the judgement to execute them well. 

There are some exceptions though, where I believe doing things slowly and carefully still matters. Non-trivial things like security, infrastructure, performance. I think it's going to be worth my time to understand these parts of software really well and honestly, this is the more fun stuff.

I'm starting to believe the only thing that can really differentiate someone now is their ideas. How they think. What choices they make. Taste (a meme at this point, but I see it). How they connect ideas across domains. In short, being highly individualistic and following your own instincts.
