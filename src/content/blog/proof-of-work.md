I'm constantly thinking about the future of work, like nearly everyone these days. AI seems like it's capable of a vast amount of white collar work, the kind equivalent to mental manual labour. 

For a software engineer, that includes tasks like writing APIs, wiring up UI, and a large number of other everyday tasks that people used to be payed by the hour for. Figuring out arcane dependency and package management issues. Debugging syntax errors. The list goes on. A lot of these kinds of tasks can be done by AI coding agents fairly well now. It doesn't matter if they don't get it 100% right the first try, a single engineer who knows what the output should be like can infact slowly prompt their way into the solutions. You can be as slow or fast as you want with AI, all this talk of one-shotting things and having agents work in parallel is a distraction in my opinion. But even going slow, which is what I try, the speed up in productivity is enormous.

But if AI can infact do so much of the small stuff by itself, and it pretty much has access to all knowledge ever written down, it genuinely re-adjusts the value provided by a single engineer. There are so many jobs where the whole point was doing the kind of plumbing work I talked about earlier. And there used to be a lot of value in being a digital plumber and knowing your pipes well. But now, what is the point? Learning exact APIs and syntaxes of random frameworks, libraries, etc (The kind you see padding every fullstack developer's resume) seems like a waste of time when the AI can just write these bits for you. I think digital plumbing work is majorly deprioritized now and that's a good thing. 

People can and should focus on the bigger picture now. Leave the small stuff to the AI. Learn concepts and know what you're doing in as much depth as possible because that's what helps to control AI and get the best out of them. I think building a solid conceptual understanding of everything and developing a strong intuition is the the only way forward. With AI implementing things, the only things that matter are having good ideas and being able to execute them to a high standard. 

There are some exceptions though, where I still believe doing things slow and careful is still important. Things like security, infrastructure, performance. I think it's going to be worth my time to understand these parts of software really well and honestly, this is the more fun stuff.

I'm starting to believe the only thing that can really differentiate someone now is their ideas. How they think. What choices they make. Taste (a meme at this point, but I see it). How they relate different ideas and make connections. In short, being highly individualistic and following that.
