---
title: Proof of humanity
description: Work and creativity in the AI age
pubDate: '2026-06-12'
---
I'm constantly thinking about the future of work, like nearly everyone these days. AI seems like it's capable of a vast amount of white collar work, the kind which was the mental equivalent of manual labour. 

For a software engineer, that means tasks like writing basic APIs, wiring up UI, etc and these can be mostly handled by AI coding agents now. It doesn't matter if they don't get it 100% right the first try, a single engineer who knows what the output should be like can infact slowly prompt their way into building what they want. You can be as slow or fast as you want with AI, all this talk of one-shotting things and having agents work in parallel is a distraction in my opinion. 

But if AI can infact do so much of the small stuff by itself, and it pretty much has access to all knowledge ever written down, what value can a single human bring? Lower level skills seem genuinely worthless. What's the point of learning exact APIs and syntax for random frameworks, libraries, etc? The kind you see padding every fullstack developers resume. I think it's useless now and that's a good thing. 

People can and should focus on the bigger picture now. Leave the small stuff to the AI. Learn concepts and know what you're doing in as much depth as possible because that's what helps the most to control AI. I think building a solid conceptual understanding of everything and developing a strong intuition is the the only way forward. With AI implementing things, the only things that matter are having good ideas and being able to execute them to a high standard. 

So the only thing that can really differentiate you is your ideas. How you think. What choices you make. Your taste. How you relate different ideas and make connections. In short, being highly individualistic.
